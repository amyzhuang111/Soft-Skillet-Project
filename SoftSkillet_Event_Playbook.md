# Soft Skillet — Trade Show Playbook
### The Complete System for Generating Maximum Pipeline from In-Person Events

---

## Why This Playbook Exists

In-person events are one of Soft Skillet's strongest growth levers. But booth conversations are short, high-stakes, and unrepeatable — a rep has one shot with each prospect. The NYC Restaurant Show produced 10 recorded conversations. Of those, only 2 resulted in a firm next step (a scheduled demo or a "call me tomorrow"). That's a 20% close rate on conversations — and several of the 8 that didn't close were high-fit prospects who left warm but uncommitted. The gap isn't interest. The gap is execution in the final 60 seconds and discipline in the first 90.

This playbook codifies every lesson from those 10 conversations into a repeatable system — so that the next event produces twice the demos booked and half the leads lost.

---

## Phase 1: Pre-Event (2+ Weeks Out)

### 1.1 Build a Tiered Target List

Don't show up and hope the right people walk by. Work the attendee and exhibitor list in advance.

**How to tier:**

| Tier | Definition | Example from NYC Show | Pre-Event Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 — Must-Meet | Enterprise groups, known dissatisfaction with incumbent, existing Soft Skillet relationship, or warm referral | Dinex Group (enterprise, OpenTable frustration), BJ's (Maria Chen referral, ~200 locations), Elizabeth Murray's group (review app power user) | Personalized 1:1 outreach to book a 15-min booth meeting at a specific time |
| 2 — Should-Meet | Mid-market operators, likely ICP fit, no prior relationship | Felice 64, Coszcal de Allende, Rustik Tavern | Batch outreach with event-specific hook |
| 3 — Walk-ups | Unknown until they arrive | Masala King, Taco Mahal | Handle with qualification framework (see 2.1) |

**For every Tier 1 account, build a one-page brief before the show:**

| Research Field | Where to Find It | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Current reservation platform | Their website ("Powered by OpenTable/Resy"), industry contacts, review app data | Determines competitive displacement angle |
| Number of locations and type | Website, press, LinkedIn | Sizes the deal and determines pitch complexity |
| Existing Soft Skillet touchpoints | Internal CRM — are they a review app user? Have they contacted support? | Existing trust = faster close. Three NYC show prospects were review app users (Daljeet, Danikkah, Elizabeth). Two of them explicitly said their trust in our review product was why they were open to the reservations pitch. This is our unfair advantage — use it. |
| Recent press, expansion plans, leadership changes | Google News, LinkedIn, industry publications | Conversation openers and signals of budget availability |
| Who's attending and what they control | Attendee list cross-referenced with LinkedIn | Determines whether you're talking to a decision-maker, influencer, or someone outside the buying center entirely |

**Pre-event outreach template (Tier 1):**

> Subject: Quick hello before [Event Name]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I saw [Company] is attending [Event] — we'll be at Booth [#] and I'd love to grab 15 minutes with you.
>
> We work with [similar restaurant type/a peer they'd recognize] on [brief, relevant value prop — e.g., "making midweek revenue more predictable" or "optimizing in-house dining revenue"]. Given [something specific about their operation — e.g., "the growth you've had this year" or "your expansion into [neighborhood]"], I think there's a conversation worth having.
>
> Would [Day 1 at 11 AM] or [Day 2 at 2 PM] work for a quick stop by? Happy to work around your schedule.
>
> — [Rep Name], Soft Skillet

Even 3-4 pre-booked meetings per day transforms event ROI. It's the difference between fishing with a net and fishing with a spear.

### 1.2 Pitch Certification

Every rep on the floor must be able to deliver all three pitches fluently and know which to deploy based on what the buyer says — not based on personal preference or comfort.

**The three pitches, with full talk tracks:**

---

**PITCH 1: OPERATOR**
*Use when the buyer's pain is emotional — frustration with vendors, feeling misunderstood, wanting a partner.*

**Core message (10 seconds):**
> "Soft Skillet was started by restaurateurs who got fed up with the exact same thing you're describing. Everything we've built comes from that frustration. It's built for operators, by operators."

**Expanded version (30 seconds):**
> "Our founders ran a successful restaurant group in Chicago. They got so sick of technology that felt like it was designed by people who'd never worked a Saturday night service that they decided to build their own. So our support team isn't a call center — it's staffed by former GMs and front-of-house managers. When you call us, you're talking to someone who knows what 'in the weeds' means."

**When it lands (from the NYC show):**
- Dee Inthamat: *"It feels like it was built by people in a lab somewhere."* → John led with Operator → Dee immediately said *"That's... that's huge"* and asked for a demo with her CEO.
- Frantz Metellus: *"The platforms we use feel like they were designed in a lab by people who've never had to deal with a surprise 12-top."* → Sam led with Operator → Frantz: *"You're singing my song."*
- Brian Krakower: *"We're really looking for a better partner... it feels like we submit a support ticket and it goes into a black hole."* → Sam should have led with Operator but led with AI Seating instead → Brian got skeptical.

**Anchor story to use with this pitch:**
> "Last month, one of our new partners told us his old provider pushed a mandatory software update at 6 PM on a Friday. Broke his entire floor map. He couldn't get a real human on the phone for two hours — during Friday dinner service."
>
> *(Pause. Let it land. The buyer will have their own version of this story.)*

This story worked extremely well when Sam used it with Frantz — it produced Frantz's most emotionally engaged response in the entire conversation. Every rep should have 2-3 anchor stories like this memorized.

---

**PITCH 2: SMOOTH REVENUE**
*Use when the buyer's pain is operational — unpredictable demand, staffing nightmares, food waste, slow nights.*

**Core message (10 seconds):**
> "We analyze your data and guarantee a minimum number of guests on your slower nights. If our prediction is wrong, we pay you the difference."

**Expanded version (30 seconds):**
> "We know how stressful it is when Friday is packed and Tuesday is a ghost town. You're guessing on produce orders, guessing on staffing — and sometimes a surprise pop catches you short. Smooth Revenue gives you a floor. We guarantee at least X guests on a given day. If we get it wrong, we write you a check. It means you can budget food costs and staff with confidence instead of hoping."

**When it lands (from the NYC show):**
- Daljeet Masala: *"Predictability. Just... predictability. The rollercoaster is killing me."* → John led with Smooth Revenue → Daljeet: *"Wait, say that again. You guarantee a minimum number of guests?"* → *"That would change everything for me. Call me tomorrow."*
- Elizabeth Murray: *"Our demand is all over the place. One Tuesday we're full, the next two we're a ghost town."* → Sam led with AI Seating instead → Elizabeth had to redirect: *"How does that help me on a slow Tuesday?"* → Sam pivoted to Smooth Revenue → Elizabeth: *"Okay, we got there. That guarantee — that's the solution I was looking for."*

**The guarantee is a "pattern interrupt."** In a show floor full of vendors saying "we optimize" and "we leverage AI," a concrete financial guarantee stops people mid-sentence. Daljeet literally said *"Wait, say that again."* This is the highest-converting single moment in any pitch — always deliver it clearly and let it breathe. Don't rush past it.

**Key detail to include:** Specify that this is especially effective for independent and small-group restaurants. It directly addresses their core anxiety — cash flow uncertainty — in a way that enterprise groups (who have more financial buffers) may feel less acutely.

---

**PITCH 3: AI SEATING**
*Use when the buyer's pain is about floor efficiency, revenue optimization, or table management.*

**Core message — PLAIN LANGUAGE ONLY (15 seconds):**
> "Imagine a really smart assistant for your host. It knows that on a Friday night, you get a lot of four-tops, but on Tuesdays it's mostly couples. So when your host is looking at the reservation list and the walk-ins at the door, the system suggests holding a specific table for a likely larger party — rather than giving a six-top to a two-person walk-in at 7:30 on a Friday."

**The analogy that works:**
> "It's like playing perfect Tetris with your reservations and tables every single night."

This exact analogy emerged organically when Fabiola said *"So it's like playing a game of Tetris with my reservations and tables to make everything fit better"* — and John confirmed it. Use it proactively. It's instantly understandable and memorable.

**NEVER lead with these phrases:**
- ~~"AI-driven optimization engine"~~
- ~~"Algorithmically determines table assignments"~~
- ~~"Dynamic learning system that optimizes your floor map in real-time to maximize per-person-average"~~
- ~~"Intelligently managing your inventory — which is your tables"~~

Every one of these was actually used at the NYC show. Fabiola thought we wanted her to physically rearrange her restaurant's furniture every night. Brian Krakower responded to the jargon version by saying *"'Optimizes' and 'AI' are both words I hear a lot from vendors. What does that actually mean?"* When prospects have to ask you to translate, you've already lost momentum.

**The rule:** If a host with 3 months of experience wouldn't understand the sentence, rewrite it.

### 1.3 Pitch Certification Drills

Before every event, run these exact role-play scenarios. A manager plays the buyer. The rep must identify the correct lead pitch, deliver it, and transition to a close — all within 90 seconds.

**Scenario 1 — The Predictability Problem:**
> *Manager (as buyer):* "Honestly, our weekends are great. But Monday through Wednesday is a complete coin flip. Last week I threw out $800 in produce because Tuesday was dead. I had six servers standing around. Then Wednesday we got slammed and couldn't keep up."
>
> *Correct response:* Lead with **Smooth Revenue**. Mirror the emotional pain ("That uncertainty is exhausting"), then deliver the guarantee. Do NOT mention AI Seating.

**Scenario 2 — The Frustrated Operator:**
> *Manager (as buyer):* "We're on Resy and honestly, I'm fed up. Their support is nonexistent. I submit a ticket and nothing happens for days. It feels like they sold us a bunch of features that don't actually work. We just want someone who gets it."
>
> *Correct response:* Lead with **Operator**. Mirror the partnership pain ("That's exactly why our founders started this company"), deliver the support story (former GMs on staff, the Friday-6PM-update anchor story). Do NOT lead with AI Seating features.

**Scenario 3 — The Floor Optimizer:**
> *Manager (as buyer):* "My takeout business is killing it, but when I look at the dining room on a busy night, it just feels... inefficient. We have tables open but they're the wrong size for the people waiting. I know we could do more volume."
>
> *Correct response:* Lead with **AI Seating** in plain language ("Imagine a smart assistant for your host..."). Use the Tetris analogy. Do NOT open with "AI-driven optimization engine."

**Scenario 4 — The Non-ICP Visitor:**
> *Manager (as buyer):* "We're a fast-casual counter-service spot. We don't take reservations. Our biggest problem is managing takeout and delivery orders."
>
> *Correct response:* Acknowledge within 2 minutes that the reservations product isn't the right fit. Ask if they use the review app. Capture their contact info for an internal handoff. Do NOT spend 10 minutes pitching AI Seating to a restaurant with no host stand.

**Scenario 5 — The Champion (Not the Decision-Maker):**
> *Manager (as buyer):* "This sounds amazing. If it looks good, I'll run it up to my Director of Operations. He normally trusts me on this stuff."
>
> *Correct response:* "That makes total sense. Let's get you a 20-minute demo first so you can see it yourself, and then we'll set up a second call together with [Director]. Does Tuesday or Thursday work better for you?" Do NOT ask to bypass them and get on the Director's calendar directly.

**Reps who default to the same pitch in 3+ scenarios are not ready for the floor.**

### 1.4 Prepare Booth Materials

**Physical materials:**
- One-pagers for each pitch (Smooth Revenue, AI Seating, Operator story) — designed to be handed over or emailed immediately post-conversation
- Business cards for every rep (obvious, but confirmed — John handed cards at the show)

**Digital materials:**
- **ROI proof points.** At minimum, one quantified case study per pitch:
  - Smooth Revenue: "[Restaurant X] reduced midweek food waste by Y% and cut over-staffing hours by Z% in the first 60 days."
  - AI Seating: "[Restaurant Y] increased per-table revenue by X% and seated Z additional covers per week without adding tables."
  - Operator: "[Restaurant Z] switched from [Resy/OpenTable] and rated our support 9.8/10 in a post-onboarding survey. Average support response time: [X minutes]."
  - At the NYC show, not a single conversation included a quantified ROI claim. Every pitch stayed conceptual. Numbers create urgency. "We help restaurants grow revenue" is forgettable. "Our average partner sees a 12% lift in midweek covers within 60 days" is actionable.

- **Scheduling QR code** printed and displayed at the booth, linking to a calendar page for demos. When a prospect won't commit to a specific time on the spot, the fallback is: "Scan this and grab any slot that works for you this week." This is faster and stickier than exchanging cards and hoping.

- **Lead capture form** (tablet or phone app) with required fields: name, title, company, email, current platform, primary pain (in their words), which pitch resonated, next step agreed, and lead temperature (Hot / Warm / Cool / Not ICP). This gets filled out within 5 minutes of each conversation — not batched at end of day.

### 1.5 Booth Staffing Strategy

With two reps (John and Sam), plan booth coverage intentionally:

- **Stagger breaks.** Never leave the booth empty. One rep takes breaks while the other covers.
- **Tag-team on Tier 1 accounts.** If a Tier 1 prospect walks up and both reps are available, one leads the conversation while the other observes. The observer can jump in for the close or handle the lead capture immediately after.
- **Specialize by strength.** Based on NYC show performance: John is stronger at discovery, emotional mirroring, and pitch sequencing. Sam is stronger at energy, rapport, and social proof. In an ideal world, John handles the discovery and Sam reinforces with enthusiasm and proof points. In practice, whoever the prospect approaches first should lead — but knowing each other's strengths allows for smarter tag-teaming.
- **Debrief between conversations.** After each substantive conversation (2+ minutes), take 60 seconds to debrief with your partner: "What did they need? What landed? Did I miss anything?" This builds real-time coaching into the event, not just in the post-mortem.

### 1.6 Set Event KPIs

Before the show, define success. Not "have good conversations" — concrete numbers.

| KPI | Target | How to Track |
|---|---|---|
| Total substantive conversations (2+ minutes) | 8-12 per rep per day | Lead capture form |
| Demos scheduled (calendar hold confirmed) | 3-5 per rep per day | Calendar |
| Warm leads (interested, no demo yet) | 3-5 per rep per day | Lead capture form |
| Not-ICP conversations exceeding 5 minutes | 0-1 per rep per day | Self-report + transcript review |
| Average conversation length | 5-8 minutes | Timestamp on lead capture |
| Cross-sell leads captured and handed off | Track all | Internal handoff log |

## Phase 2: During the Event

### 2.1 The First 90 Seconds: Qualify Before You Pitch

The biggest temptation at a booth is to start talking about the product. Resist it. The first 90 seconds should be almost entirely the buyer talking.

**Opening framework:**

> **"Hey, welcome. I'm [name] with Soft Skillet. What brought you to the show this year?"**
> *(Let them answer. Don't interrupt.)*
>
> **"What's the biggest challenge you're trying to solve right now?"**
> *(Let them answer. Listen for pain signals. Take a mental note of their exact words — you'll use them in the close and the follow-up email.)*

That's it. Two questions. Their answers tell you:
- Whether they're ICP (do they have a reservations/seating challenge?)
- Which pitch to lead with (predictability → Smooth Revenue; frustration with tech → Operator; floor efficiency → AI Seating)
- How urgent their pain is (are they actively looking to switch, or just browsing?)
- Whether they're a decision-maker or an influencer (owner/CIO vs. AGM/Director who needs to "run it by" someone)

**The power of mirroring:**

When Daljeet said *"Predictability... the rollercoaster is killing me,"* John didn't immediately pitch. He reflected it back: *"It's the uncertainty, right? Not just the slow nights themselves."* Daljeet lit up: *"Exactly!"* That single moment of mirroring accomplished three things:
1. It made the buyer feel deeply understood
2. It sharpened the pain from a vague complaint into a specific emotional problem (uncertainty, not just slow nights)
3. It set up Smooth Revenue as the exact, inevitable answer

**This is the model.** Before you pitch, prove you heard them. Reflect their pain back in slightly sharper language. Then — and only then — introduce the solution.

**What bad discovery sounds like (actual example):**

Brian Krakower said: *"We're really looking for a better partner. Our current provider is Resy, and it feels like we submit a support ticket and it goes into a black hole. They sold us on all these features that don't quite work as advertised."*

Sam responded: *"I hear you. You want tech that actually delivers on its promises. That's our AI Seating product in a nutshell. It's a dynamic, learning system that optimizes your floor map in real-time to maximize per-person-average and table turn times."*

The buyer asked for empathy and partnership. He got a feature spec for a product he didn't ask about. The pitch wasn't wrong in isolation — it was wrong for that moment. The right response would have been:

> *"That's incredibly frustrating — and honestly, that's the exact reason our founders started this company. They were restaurant operators who felt the same way about their tech. So we built the support model differently. Our team is staffed by former GMs and FOH managers. When you call us, you're not talking to a script — you're talking to someone who's lived a Saturday night service. Can I tell you how that looks in practice?"*

Lead with what they asked for. Layer in product after trust is established.

### 2.2 The Matching Framework: Pain Determines Pitch

This is the single most important rule in this playbook:

> **The buyer's pain determines the pitch sequence. The rep does not choose.**

After discovery, match the buyer's language to the right lead pitch:

| If the buyer says something like... | Lead with... | Then layer in... | NYC Show Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| "I can't predict demand" / "Slow nights are killing us" / "I'm guessing on staffing every week" / "I'm throwing out food I can't use" | **Smooth Revenue** — the guarantee is a pattern interrupt that immediately creates urgency | Operator (empathy), then AI Seating if they also mention floor issues | Daljeet: "The rollercoaster is killing me" → Smooth Revenue → "Call me tomorrow" |
| "Our tech feels outdated" / "Nobody understands restaurants" / "We want a real partner, not a vendor" / "Support goes into a black hole" | **Operator** — the founder story and support model build emotional trust before any product enters the conversation | Smooth Revenue or AI Seating based on their operational pain | Dee: "It was built by people in a lab" → Operator → "That's huge. Get Daniel in for a demo." |
| "We're leaving money on the table" / "Our floor is inefficient" / "We could do more volume in-house" / "Wrong tables for the people waiting" | **AI Seating** — but ONLY in plain language using the host-assistant framing and Tetris analogy | Operator (credibility), then Smooth Revenue if midweek pain exists | Fabiola: "It just feels inefficient" → AI Seating (after jargon recovery) → understood |
| Pain is outside reservations (takeout, delivery, POS integration, kitchen ops) | **Don't force it.** Acknowledge the pain honestly. See 2.3. | — | Kelly: "#1 pain is takeout/delivery" → John pitched reservations anyway → vague "next month" brush-off |
| "We don't take reservations" / "We're counter-service" / "We don't have a host" | **This is not ICP.** Exit gracefully within 3 minutes. See 2.3. | — | Danikkah: "We're fast-casual, mostly counter-service" → Sam kept pitching → wasted time + missed cross-sell |

**Pitch layering — the optimal sequence when multiple pains are present:**

Many buyers will express more than one pain. The NYC show revealed a clear hierarchy for which pitch to lead with:

1. **If the buyer mentions uncertainty/predictability at all → lead with Smooth Revenue.** The guarantee is the most differentiated, most emotionally resonant, and highest-converting single moment in our pitch arsenal. It stops people mid-sentence. Everything else can come after.
2. **If the buyer mentions vendor frustration but not predictability → lead with Operator.** Build the emotional trust foundation before introducing any product features.
3. **Lead with AI Seating only when the buyer specifically describes a floor optimization problem** and has not mentioned predictability or vendor frustration. AI Seating is the hardest pitch to land at a booth because it's the most technical and the most easily misunderstood.

At the NYC show, Sam led with AI Seating in 4 of 5 conversations. In every case, it either confused the buyer (Brian: *"What does that actually mean for my host?"*), missed the real pain (Elizabeth: *"But how does that help me on a slow Tuesday?"*), or was irrelevant to the buyer's operation (Danikkah: *"We don't really have seating charts"*). AI Seating is a powerful product — but it should almost never be the opening pitch at an event.

### 2.3 The 3-Minute Rule: Qualify Out Fast, Hand Off Warm

Not every person at the booth is a prospect for the reservations product. Spending 10 minutes with a non-ICP visitor costs you the enterprise buyer who walked past while you were occupied.

**Disqualifying signals (recognize within 3 minutes):**
- No reservations system and no need for one (counter-service, fast-casual, walk-in only with no host)
- Pain is entirely outside product scope (takeout/delivery, POS integration, kitchen throughput)
- No authority or influence over reservation tech decisions and can't connect you to someone who does

**When you identify a non-fit, exit gracefully:**

> *"It sounds like [takeout management / kitchen throughput / X] is your real challenge right now, and I want to be honest with you — our reservations product isn't the right tool for that. I don't want to waste your time on something that won't solve your actual problem. But I'd love to stay connected because [we're expanding our product line / I think there could be a fit down the road / I know people in that space I could connect you with]. Can I grab your email?"*

This does three things: it respects their time (they'll remember that), it preserves the relationship for future product expansion, and it frees you for the next conversation.

**The cross-sell protocol — do NOT skip this:**

At the NYC show, Danikkah Josan (Taco Mahal) was not ICP for reservations — but she was an existing review app user who explicitly said *"I don't think we're currently getting the most out of your reviews product... I'm sure we could be doing more."* Sam's response: *"If you go on our website there's a contact form and I'm sure one of my colleagues would love to help you out."*

This is one of the worst outcomes possible from an event conversation. An existing, enthusiastic customer came to us asking for help — and we sent her to a generic web form. The conversion rate from "website contact form" to "actual conversation with a human" is low. The conversion rate from "warm internal handoff with context" is high.

**The rule: Every lead leaves the booth with a human connection, not a URL.**

If the prospect needs a different team:
1. Capture their name, email, company, and what they need (write it down on the spot)
2. Tell them: *"I'll personally connect you with [name/team] — they'll reach out within a day or two with context so you don't have to repeat yourself."*
3. Send the internal handoff email or Slack message within 24 hours with full context: who they are, what they use, what they need, and the fact that they came to us at the event. This is a warm lead for another team — treat it like one.

### 2.4 The Existing-Customer Advantage

Three of the ten NYC show prospects were already Soft Skillet review app users: Daljeet (Masala King), Danikkah (Taco Mahal), and Elizabeth Murray. Two of the three explicitly cited their trust in the review product as the reason they were willing to consider the reservations product:

- Daljeet: *"I already trust your company from the other app, and this is exactly what I need."*
- Elizabeth: *"Your review product has been so fantastic and reliable for us that it's earned you guys a lot of trust. I'm willing to bet you've gotten the reservations part right, too."*

This is Soft Skillet's single biggest competitive advantage at events. No competitor has this — an existing product relationship that has already built trust through daily use.

**How to leverage it:**

1. **Pre-event:** Pull a list of review app users in the event's geographic area. Cross-reference with the attendee list. These are automatic Tier 1 targets — they already know us and like us.
2. **At the booth:** If someone identifies themselves as a review app user, acknowledge it immediately and build on it:
   > *"That's great to hear — our review team works incredibly hard on that product. What I love is that the same people, the same philosophy of support, built our reservations platform. If you've had a good experience with us on the review side, I think you'll find the same thing here."*
3. **In the pitch:** You can move faster with existing users. They don't need the Operator pitch as much — they've already experienced the culture. Go straight to the product that matches their pain.
4. **In the close:** Reference the existing relationship as a reason to move quickly: *"Since you already know how we operate, this should be a pretty smooth evaluation. Want to do a 20-minute demo this week?"*

### 2.5 Handling Objections

The NYC show surfaced four recurring objections. Every rep should have a prepared response for each.

---

**OBJECTION 1: "AI makes decisions? I don't want to take control away from my hosts."**

*This came from Brian Krakower (Convo 2): "I'm just skeptical about taking that control away from our hosts. They know our regulars, they can read the crowd."*

**Wrong response (what happened):**
> *"It's definitely a tool for the hosts! It makes their lives easier. And it makes your life easier when you see the bottom-line impact."*

This dismisses the concern without addressing it. Brian raised a real tension between automation and human judgment — and Sam waved it away with a generic reassurance.

**Right response:**
> *"That's a great instinct, and I want to be really clear — the system doesn't replace your hosts. It's a suggestion engine. Think of it as a copilot who whispers in their ear: 'Hey, Table 12 might be worth holding for ten minutes — there's a high chance a four-top is about to walk in.' Your host still makes the call. They still greet the regulars, read the room, override when their gut says to. We just give them better information to work with. The best hosts we've seen actually love it because it takes the mental math off their plate so they can focus on hospitality."*

**Why this works:** It validates the concern, clarifies the human-AI relationship with a concrete example, and reframes the tool as an enhancement to hospitality — not a replacement of it.

---

**OBJECTION 2: "We're already on [OpenTable/Resy] and it's... fine."**

*This came from Kelly MacPherson (Convo 5): "We've been with Resy for a long time, and honestly, it's fine. There's nothing glaringly wrong with it."*

**Wrong response:** Immediately pitching features of your product. If they say the incumbent is "fine," they're not in active pain — and a feature list won't create it.

**Right response:**
> *"'Fine' is interesting. I hear that a lot, actually. Usually what it means is the system checks a box but doesn't feel like a partner. It doesn't actively make your business better — it just... exists. Is that close to how you'd describe it?"*

**Why this works:** It reframes "fine" from a barrier ("nothing's wrong, so why switch?") into an opening ("fine isn't good enough"). If they agree, you've established that the incumbent is underperforming against what's possible — and now you can introduce Soft Skillet as the upgrade. John actually started well on this — *"It checks a box but doesn't feel like a partner"* — but then failed to build on it because Kelly's real pain (takeout/delivery) didn't match the product.

**Follow-up if they agree "fine" isn't great:**
> *"So the question is less about what's broken and more about what you're missing. Let me ask — if you could wave a magic wand and change one thing about your restaurant operations right now, what would it be?"*

This reopens discovery and may reveal a pain you can address — or may confirm the pain is outside your product, in which case you qualify out gracefully (see 2.3).

---

**OBJECTION 3: "This sounds great, but changing systems for a company our size is a big operation."**

*This came from Brian Krakower (Convo 2): "Making a change like this for a company of our size is a big operation and takes a lot of trust."*

**Right response:**
> *"Absolutely — and that's exactly why we wouldn't propose flipping a switch across 200 locations overnight. The way our most successful enterprise partners have rolled this out is with a focused pilot: pick 3-5 locations that represent different service patterns, run for 60 days, measure the results, and then make a data-driven decision on expansion. No risk, no disruption, and you get real numbers from your own restaurants — not a vendor's slide deck. Would it make sense to talk through what a pilot like that could look like for BJ's?"*

**Why this works:** It de-risks the decision by introducing a pilot framework. Enterprise buyers are rarely afraid of the product — they're afraid of the migration. Show them you understand the implementation complexity and have a staged approach.

---

**OBJECTION 4: "I need to run this by [my boss / my Director of Ops / Daniel]."**

*This came from Dee (Convo 1), Orla (Convo 10), Elizabeth (Convo 7), and Karin (Convo 6) — it's the most common objection at events.*

**See Section 2.6 for the full stakeholder handling framework.** Short version: arm the champion, don't bypass them.

### 2.6 Stakeholder Mapping: Identify and Empower

Every booth conversation should answer two questions by the halfway point:

1. **Is this person the decision-maker?**
2. **If not, who is — and what's the path to them?**

**How to find out (without being awkward about it):**

Don't ask "Are you the decision-maker?" — it's condescending and puts people on the defensive. Instead:

> *"If this looked like a fit, what would the evaluation process look like on your end?"*

Or, after they express interest:

> *"Who else on your team would need to weigh in on something like this?"*

These questions surface the buying process naturally. At the NYC show, the answers fell into three scenarios:

---

**SCENARIO A — Decision-maker is at the booth.**

Examples: Daljeet (owner, Masala King), Frantz (owner, Rustik Tavern).

These people can say yes on the spot. Move from discovery to close without unnecessary layers. Daljeet literally said "Forget next week. Call me tomorrow." Don't slow down a buyer who's ready to move.

**SCENARIO B — Influencer/champion is at the booth, decision-maker is elsewhere.**

Examples: Dee Inthamat (Dinex — needs Daniel Boulud), Orla Josifi (Felice 64 — needs Director of Ops David), Elizabeth Murray (needs her GMs to evaluate), Frantz (needs his manager Victoria for day-to-day).

This is the most common scenario at events — and the one Soft Skillet handled most inconsistently. The rule:

> **Arm the champion. Never bypass them.**

**What "arming the champion" looks like in practice:**

| Step | Action | Example Language |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Validate their role | Acknowledge that them evaluating first is the smart move | *"That makes total sense — you should absolutely see it first before bringing it to [name]."* |
| 2. Schedule THEIR demo | Book a demo for the champion, not the decision-maker | *"Let's do a 20-minute walkthrough for you this week. Does Tuesday at 2 or Thursday at 10 work better?"* |
| 3. Equip them to sell internally | Offer to provide a summary or one-pager they can forward | *"After the demo, I'll send you a one-page summary with the key points and numbers — something you can forward to [name] with your recommendation."* |
| 4. Plan the stakeholder meeting | Set the expectation that a second meeting will follow | *"And if it looks like a fit, we'll set up a second call together with [name] so they can ask questions directly."* |

**What "bypassing the champion" looks like — and why it fails:**

In Convo 10, Orla Josifi was excited. She said: *"This sounds amazing for us... If it looks good I'll run it by my Director of Operations. He normally trusts me a lot on this stuff."*

Sam's response: *"Right, but it's probably more efficient if we just get him on the initial call... What's the best way to get a meeting on his calendar?"*

Orla's energy collapsed: *"Uh, well, I'm not sure. I don't really have access to his calendar like that."*

Sam pushed again: *"Could you make an email introduction for me to David?"*

Orla, now deflated: *"I... I guess I can see. I'll have to check on the best way to do that."*

What happened: Sam turned a warm, enthusiastic advocate into a reluctant, uncertain participant. Orla came in saying *"this sounds amazing"* and left saying *"I guess I can see."* The champion felt skipped, disrespected, and put in an awkward position — being asked to give calendar access and make introductions for a product she hadn't even evaluated yet.

**The cost:** Instead of two scheduled meetings (one with Orla, one with Orla + David), the outcome was zero meetings and a vague "I'll look into it." This is the single most avoidable loss from the entire NYC show.

**SCENARIO C — Person at the booth has no direct path to the decision-maker.**

Example: Karin Burroughs (Private Events Director, Le Bernardin) — she doesn't manage the reservation system; the GM (Chloé) does.

"I'll pass along your card" has a near-zero conversion rate. The card goes into a pocket, the pocket goes into a bag, and the bag sits in a closet. Push gently for a warmer handoff:

> *"I appreciate that, Karin. Would you be open to a quick email introduction instead? I can send you a two-line blurb you can forward — something like 'Hey Chloé, saw this at the show and thought it was worth a look.' That way she has context and I'm not cold-emailing her out of the blue."*

If they agree, send the draft intro email within hours — that night or the next morning. Make it so easy that all they have to do is hit forward. If you wait three days, the moment is gone.

**Additional opportunity John missed with Karin:** She manages private events at Le Bernardin and described a clear space-configuration challenge (*"trying to configure the space and keep all our existing reservations"*). John didn't explore whether AI Seating could serve as a wedge into Le Bernardin through the private events workflow. This may or may not have product fit — but the question was worth asking: *"That space configuration challenge you described — is that something your team deals with regularly? Our AI does something similar for the main dining room; I'd be curious whether it could help on the events side too."* Even if the answer is no, it shows genuine curiosity about her world.

### 2.8 Conversation Time Management

Based on the NYC show transcripts, conversations ranged from approximately 3 to 12 minutes. The optimal booth conversation is **5-8 minutes:**

| Phase | Duration | What Happens |
|---|---|---|
| Open + qualify | 60-90 seconds | Greeting, two discovery questions, listen |
| Mirror + match | 30-60 seconds | Reflect their pain, transition to the right pitch |
| Pitch | 60-90 seconds | Deliver the lead pitch in plain language |
| Handle questions/objections | 60-90 seconds | Address concerns, layer a second pitch if relevant |
| Close | 60 seconds | Restate pain, propose demo, lock time |

**Conversations shorter than 3 minutes** usually mean you qualified out (good) or the prospect wasn't engaged (nothing you can do).

**Conversations longer than 10 minutes** usually mean one of three things:
1. You're pitching multiple products without the buyer asking (bad)
2. The conversation drifted into storytelling or rapport-building that isn't advancing toward a next step (moderate — see Karin/Convo 6 where Drake anecdotes consumed time)
3. The buyer is deeply engaged and asking detailed questions (good — let this run, but still close)

**The signal to watch for:** If you're past 8 minutes and haven't proposed a next step, you're overextending. Transition to the close immediately regardless of where you are in the pitch.

### 2.9 Pricing Discipline: Never Discount Unprompted

At the NYC show, Sam told Brian Krakower (BJ's — ~200 locations): *"I'm sure if we move quickly enough I can also get finance to approve a discount."*

Brian had not asked about pricing. He had not expressed any hesitation about cost. He had simply said the evaluation would take trust. Sam volunteered a discount as a sweetener — before the buyer even asked.

**Why this is costly:**
1. It signals that the list price isn't real — that discounts are freely available.
2. It anchors the negotiation before it starts. Brian now expects a discount as the baseline, not a concession.
3. It reframes the relationship from "trusted partner" to "vendor trying to close a deal."
4. For a ~200-location chain, even a small unnecessary discount is significant revenue left on the table.

**The rule: Never mention pricing concessions until the buyer raises price as a specific barrier — and even then, lead with value first.**

If a buyer says *"We're paying X to Resy and it's expensive":*
> *"I understand. Let me ask — if the product delivered [specific ROI outcome], would the investment make sense at a similar price point? Because what we're really talking about is whether you're getting value for what you spend, not just what the number is."*

If after a full evaluation the buyer needs pricing flexibility to close, that's a negotiation — and it happens in the sales cycle, not at the booth.

### 2.10 Closing: The Last 60 Seconds Are Everything

This is where the NYC show left the most pipeline on the table. Conversations that went well for 8 minutes ended with "I'll email you some info" — and that's not a next step, it's a slow fade.

**The closing sequence (execute in every conversation):**

**Step 1 — Restate the pain in their words.**
> *"So it sounds like the biggest thing for you is [their exact language — 'the Tuesday rollercoaster' / 'getting a partner who actually understands restaurants' / 'making the floor work harder']."*

Using their exact words — not your paraphrase — confirms alignment and makes them feel heard one final time. It also primes them to say yes to anything that solves that specific problem.

**Step 2 — Bridge to a specific, personalized next step.**
> *"The best way to see how we solve that is a 20-minute demo. I can show you exactly how [Smooth Revenue / AI Seating] would work for [their restaurant name — Masala King, Rustik Tavern, etc.]."*

Always use their restaurant's name. "A demo of our platform" is generic. "A demo showing how Smooth Revenue would work for Masala King's Tuesday nights" is specific and feels tailored.

**Step 3 — Offer a binary choice of times.**
> *"I've got Tuesday at 2 or Thursday at 10 — which works better for you?"*

A binary choice is psychologically easier to say yes to than an open-ended "when are you free?" It also creates a subtle urgency — these slots are specific and presumably limited.

**Step 4 — If they resist a specific time:**
> *"Totally fair — I know you've got a lot going on after the show. Let me send you a calendar link right now so you can grab whatever slot works."*

Pull out your phone. Send the calendar link while you're both standing there. A link sent in-person while the conversation is fresh has dramatically higher booking rates than one sent in a follow-up email two days later. If you have the scheduling QR code printed at the booth, even better — have them scan it on the spot.

**Step 5 — Absolute floor (if they won't commit to anything):**
Confirm their email address (spell it back), tell them you'll send something specific within 24 hours, and reference the exact pain point:

> *"I'll send you a quick note tomorrow specifically about [the predictability problem / the support experience / the floor optimization piece] — not a generic brochure. Sound good?"*

This is the minimum acceptable outcome — not the goal.

## Phase 3: Post-Event (The 72-Hour Window)

Event momentum has a half-life. Within 72 hours, the prospect has been back to work for two days, dealt with three delivery app crises, had a line cook quit, and forgotten which booth they were excited about. Speed and specificity are everything.

### 3.1 Follow-Up Tiers and Timing

| Temperature | Definition | Follow-Up Action | Deadline | NYC Show Examples |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Hot** | Demo scheduled, or buyer said "call me tomorrow" / "let's get this rolling" | Confirm the calendar hold via email. If they said call, call — don't email. | **Warm** | Interested, engaged, expressed clear pain, but no meeting locked | Personalized email referencing their specific pain + calendar link with 2-3 time options | **Within 24 hours** | Brian (BJ's), Frantz (Rustik), Elizabeth Murray, Fabiola (Coszcal) |

**Follow-up email rules:**
- Keep the body under 120 words. If you can't, you're re-pitching — and the pitch already happened at the booth.
- No attachments in the first email (except for champion handoffs where a one-pager helps them sell internally).
- No "just circling back" or "just wanted to follow up." These phrases are filler and signal low status.
- No pricing, discounts, or concessions in the follow-up. The goal is to get a meeting, not negotiate.
- Reference something specific from the conversation — their exact words, their restaurant name, their specific pain. This proves you listened and separates you from every other vendor who sends a generic "great meeting you!" email.
- **Subject lines reference their pain, not your product.** *"The Tuesday night guessing game"* beats *"Thanks for visiting the Soft Skillet booth!"*

### 3.3 Second Follow-Up (If No Response in 5 Business Days)

If your first email gets no reply within 5 business days, send one more. Not a "just checking in" email — add new value.

> **Subject:** Re: [Original Subject]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> Wanted to share a quick data point that made me think of your [specific pain]:
>
> [One-sentence proof point — e.g., "One of our partners, a [similar restaurant type], reduced their midweek food waste by 30% in the first 60 days on Smooth Revenue."]
>
> Still happy to show you how this would work for [restaurant name] if the timing is better now. [Calendar link]
>
> [Rep Name]

**After two emails with no response: stop.** Don't become noise. Add them to a nurture list for quarterly check-ins or future event outreach.

### 3.4 Internal Debrief (Within 48 Hours of Event End)

Run a structured 60-minute debrief with every rep who worked the event. This is not optional.

**Agenda:**

**1. Pipeline review (15 min)**
- How many total conversations? (Target: 8-12 per rep per day)
- How many demos scheduled? (Measure against target of 3-5 per rep per day)
- How many warm leads (interested, no demo)?
- How many not-ICP?
- Conversion rate: demos scheduled / total conversations
- Total estimated pipeline value (based on company size and temperature)

**2. Pitch effectiveness (15 min)**
- Which pitch led to the most demos? (Hypothesis: Operator and Smooth Revenue for independent/small groups.)
- Which pitch misfired most often? (Hypothesis: AI Seating when deployed against wrong pain or with jargon.)
- Were there buyer pains we couldn't address? (Track these — they're product roadmap signals. At the NYC show, takeout/delivery integration was a recurring pain with no product answer.)
- Which anchor stories or proof points landed? Which fell flat?

**3. Rep-level coaching (20 min)**
- For each rep: one specific thing to keep doing, one specific thing to change. Ground both in transcript evidence.
- Review the 2 strongest and 2 weakest transcript clips per rep.
- Ask each rep: "Which conversation do you wish you could redo? What would you do differently?"

**4. Process improvements (10 min)**
- Did pre-event outreach generate pre-booked meetings? How many? Were they higher quality than walk-ups?
- Was lead capture fast enough? Were any details lost?
- What booth materials were missing or underused?
- What would we change for the next show?

**Document everything.** The debrief outputs feed directly into pre-event prep for the next show. Create a shared document with findings, and review it during the next event's pitch certification session. Lessons not recorded are lessons repeated.

### 3.5 CRM Hygiene (Within 72 Hours)

Every lead from the event should be in the CRM within 72 hours with:
- All fields from the lead capture form (see 2.11)
- Follow-up status (email sent, demo scheduled, no response, etc.)
- Assigned owner (the rep who had the conversation — continuity matters)
- Next action date

Leads without a next action date are dead leads. If there's no scheduled follow-up, the lead will disappear into the pipeline and never be touched again.
